A big structure depends on small repetitive units, the functioning of the smaller piece within the bigger whole. As a kid I started carrying bricks at my grandfathers construction site. I did so for €20 a day when I was about 12 years old. Somewhere during the day my grandfather would drive by give me €20 in cash and that would be the pay for the day. In my eyes that was a lot of money. Now it turns out that it wasn’t. As I got older I went from transportation of the bricks to the transformation of them. Stacking them, applying mortar and stacking more. It is fascinating how a small component like that starts to shape environments and creates whole structures. Somewhere in the process of stacking bricks a wall gets created, but then how many bricks does it take until we lose sight of the individual brick and start seeing it as a wall.

This question is not just architecture it is also about the self, and how the perception of ‘you’ is created. The brick serves here as a metaphor for the basic units of identity, labor, memory, and material practice. To work with bricks is to engage with a modular system: standardized, repeatable, but never identical. Bricks weather. They carry character. They are hard, rigid, and everywhere. Yet to understand shape and function to claim any form of autonomy within such a system. You must strip it of its defining elements and rebuild it elsewhere.

My attempt was to modify the brick, casting them in plaster ,, firing them in ceramics and eventually casting them in liquid porcelain. Porcelain in contrast to brick is unforgiving, get the mixture and the timing wrong and they'll collapse , fold completely inwards. In unfired state porcelain is incredibly fragile and they’ll break at the slightest interaction. In two months only 6/7 seven succeeded, resembling perfection, in beautiful white. Seven bricks might not be enough to build a wall, but it is an assembly, they were evidence of a transformed relationship to material. In altering the brick, I attempted to alter the self. The brick became a metaphor not only for origin but for change. Coming from an environment where I felt clogged up most of the time and stuck in the mundane-ness of it all most of my adult life had become an attempt at escaping it, at finding some sort of freedom. But it is ironic that freedom needs structure and regulation to start existing.

For me it was either art or escapism, I ended up trying a combination of both. Laying bricks was one thing, understanding art and doing it was another. To do art for me was to first pause, observe the existing structure the work will need to start inhabiting. After that it is mainly about intuition. A design course at a prestigious art school will try to challenge or make you question your intuition, yet as a artist it is the most important instrument you have and you ought to hold on to it tightly. It is what makes you different from an AI, or the other person. This year was the first year I started following my intuition, in the things I was making and researching, it came as a great relief.

I started noticing that most art i was drawn to and that inspired me where commenting on notions of freedom or notions of freedom. The first work I saw that embodied that was a awfully cruel one. 'dogs that cannot touch each other' . Dogs that cannot touch each other is a art installation by Chinese art duo San Yuan and Peng Yu. A couple that operate their practice from Beijing. It is dogs on treadmills running towards each other in a frenzy but never able to touch one another. The cruelty of the artwork in combination with a mostly passive audience hit me as dystopian, unsure what the art work was trying to tell me (cause art should always tell something?!?!?) At least the movement of the whole thing was impressive, the cruelty of it too, is that artistic freedom? Another work in the same line, even though passive, still, dead, in that sense I saw at Museum Angewandte Kunst in Frankfurt am Main. The exhibition is called ‘Positions on Freedom’ and it was curated and assembled with an international open call. The work was a simple wooden chair the one you’ll see in a second hand store. On the seating there was a metal arch attached, the type you’ll see in hostile architecture. A simple almost ready-made work connecting the themes of domestic and public space with comfort and familiarity.

Artist San Yuan, makes a linguistic observation that inspired me in an interview with art critic David Borgonjon: looking up “freedom” in a dictionary redirects the reader to other words (liberty, autonomy, independence). These words, in turn, refer back to freedom or to each other. The reasoning is circular. For San Yuan, this circularity is not a failure of definition but a sign that freedom is a fundamental concept - a term so basic that it cannot be decomposed into simpler, non-circular terms. He continues: “What’s God? It’s freedom, it’s to be what you will, to do as you wish. God said, let there be light, and there was light. So if you want to put this into practice, how can you not be what you will?” He aims to explain the circulair and constraining definition of freedom through an art installation called 'freedom' a huge metal room, and a fire hose that under high pressure tries to free it’s way out of it.

How can we define what freedom is if there is no set definition of it. Yesterday after work I was sitting in the park smoking cigarettes with a friend. We found ourselves in conversation with a stranger next to us, about our age, we spoke of similar concepts and the lack of time and money being young and how irrelevant our struggles actually turn out to be. And it was in a light tone that he said “Most people think that freedom is to move freely, but you can also move freely inside a box” A box has walls. A box has limits. Yet within those limits, movement is possible. Freedom might require the box to have any meaning. A completely unbounded space is not freedom; it is disorientation. To move freely in a box is to understand the box’s dimensions, to test them, to rearrange oneself within them. The box is not the opposite of freedom; it is its condition, it is the structure that speaks the option of freedom into existence.

The brick wall, is a box of bricks. The individual brick, insignificant, regains significance when the wall is questioned, altered, or built differently. The porcelain bricks are a failed wall but a successful box: a small, fragile container for autonomy and a start for defining freedom.
